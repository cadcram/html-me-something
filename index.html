East Asia in a Year!

"[East Asia] is a sleeping giant. Let her sleep, for when she wakes she will move the world." After I graduated college and I considered how I would make an impact on this world, this quote from Napoleon Bonaparte resonanted in my mind. I heard this quote years prior as a student when I spent a summer in East Asia serving with a campus ministry that I was a part of called Cru. My experience that Summer was deeply impactful to my my faith and my growth as a person. As a result, I decided that I would spend a year working with this campus ministry in East Asia.

They say that crossing cultures is hard and they are usually right! That is is why you never go alone. In the photo above is a picture of my team that would be working with for the next year. With the exception of a few people, I had never met these people, but within a couple days they became good friends, whom I still keep in contact.

A significant portion of my time in East Asia was working with Cru on college campuses. For those unfamiliar with campus ministries, my responsiblities included meeting students, building relationships, bringing up spirtual conversation, and facilitating community. As someone who worked with Cru in the States and in East Asia, the East Asia culture requires a majority of time spent in building relationships. The photo above is of a group hangout time that several coworkers and me set up to continue to build relationship with our East Asian friends. I became very good friends with some of these students despite cultural differenece and miss them very much.

East Asia holds thousands of years of rich history. Sites as pictured above are over 600 years, which is about three time as old as the United States. I enjoyed going to these sites and learning about history and about the culture that hosted me over course of the year. Although this joy soon became over shadowed by the idiosyncrasies of the East Asia people. These historical sites not only attract international vistors, but many East Asians who have never seen a foreigner. As a result, every East Asian from the boonies wants to take a picture with you. Trust me, it becomes much less flattering after the fifth time.

The biggest holiday in East Asia is Lunar New Year. Many people go home during this time of year to visit family and celebrate. As a result, the city I was in was very empty and many businesses shut down, including grocery stores. In order to stay alive, my team and I were able to travel to Thailand. This is THE beach destination in all of Asia. It was much needed solace because where I lived in East Asia was very cold and very poluted. The sunshine and clear blue skies can rejuvenate the mind and the body.

Many people travel the world for the sites and experiences, but the year I spent in East Asia made me realize that what give most expereinces their meaning is the relationships that you form on the way. A photo below shows my roommates and I on a trip that we took to Hong Kong. This is only weeks before we went home and parted ways, so we spent it exploring an exciting place together! Below that photo, is my last Sunday at the church I attended, which served as an important community of faith that guided me through this year full of new stresses and confusion and pointed me to God, who is our peace.

There is many more things to say about this year I spent in East Asia. The sights, the smells, which are many in this country, but most importantly the people I met along the way would take ages to explain, but I hope this gave you a quick snapshot of my expereince. Thanks for reading!

Note: I refer to the location that I visited as East Asia and concealed the idenity of nationals due to the nature of my work there.
